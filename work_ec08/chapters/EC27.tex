\begin{engineeringnews}
At dimensions below about one hundred nanometres, the ratio between surface area and volume rises sharply and quantum confinement can modify optical or electrical behaviour. Nanotechnology exploits these scale effects in electronics, coatings, sensors, medicine and energy systems.
\end{engineeringnews}

\begin{ecarticle}
A nanometre is one billionth of a metre. A material at this scale may behave differently from the same substance in bulk form. The first reason is geometric: for similar particles, surface area varies with the square of size whereas volume varies with the cube. The surface-area-to-volume ratio therefore increases as the characteristic length decreases. Surface reactions, interfaces and adhesion can then dominate the behaviour of the whole object.

The second reason is quantum confinement. When electrons are restricted to dimensions comparable with their characteristic wavelengths, allowed energy levels change. Quantum dots consequently emit different colours depending on particle size, and nanoscale conductors can show electrical properties that do not appear in larger components.

Engineers use two broad manufacturing strategies. Top-down processes remove material from a larger structure by lithography, etching or precision machining. They provide accurate patterns and compatibility with semiconductor production, but become expensive as feature size decreases. Bottom-up processes assemble atoms, molecules or nanoparticles through chemical synthesis, deposition or self-organisation. They can create extremely small structures over large areas, although controlling defects, orientation and particle-size distribution is difficult.

Nanomaterials include particles, tubes, wires, thin films and porous structures. Carbon nanotubes combine low density with high axial stiffness and electrical conductivity. Nanostructured catalysts expose many active sites, so less precious metal may be required. Thin coatings can alter friction, corrosion resistance, wettability or optical reflection without changing the bulk component.

Measurement and quality control are major engineering challenges. Conventional optical microscopes cannot resolve features much smaller than the wavelength of visible light. Electron microscopy, atomic-force microscopy and spectroscopy are therefore used to measure dimensions, morphology and composition. Manufacturing must also limit agglomeration, contamination and variation between batches.

Benefits must be assessed together with risks. Nanoparticles may enter organisms and ecosystems through pathways that are less important for larger particles. Toxicity depends on size, shape, surface chemistry and solubility, not only on total mass. Responsible design therefore includes containment, ventilation, exposure monitoring, recycling and end-of-life planning.
\end{ecarticle}

For a spherical particle of diameter $d$, the surface-area-to-volume ratio is $6/d$. Halving the diameter therefore doubles this ratio.

\begin{engineeringchallenge}
A coating contains spherical nanoparticles with a diameter of $40\,\mathrm{nm}$. The same material is also available as particles with a diameter of $200\,\mathrm{nm}$.
\begin{enumerate}
\item Calculate the surface-area-to-volume ratio $6/d$ for both particle sizes.
\item Determine how many times larger the ratio is for the $40\,\mathrm{nm}$ particles.
\item Explain how this change could improve catalytic activity but also increase exposure risk.
\item Propose one measurement method and two exposure-control measures for production.
\end{enumerate}
\end{engineeringchallenge}
